### What the Space Says About Her

**Minimalism as Control:**
The apartment is conspicuously sparse. This isn't minimalist design aesthetic - it's active avoidance of permanence. Kate has lived here three years but it looks like she could pack and leave in an afternoon. No art, no photos displayed, no decorative objects. The only personal item visible is a single framed photo on the bedroom dresser (Kate, Mike, and mom from Mike's college graduation - facing the wall, not outward).

**Functional Over Comfortable:**
Every item serves a purpose. The couch is for sitting while eating or streaming, not for lounging. The bed is for sleeping, not reading (she reads at desk or on couch). The kitchen is for making coffee and reheating leftovers, not cooking elaborate meals. This reflects her broader approach - life is about execution, not enjoyment.

**The Coffee Exception:**
The only space that shows care is the coffee setup. The Chemex sits prominently, filters stocked, grinder clean, kettle polished. This morning ritual is sacred - the one area where she allows herself precision and attention. Everything else in the apartment could disappear and she'd barely notice. The Chemex stays.

**Absence of John:**
After the breakup (September 2024), there's nothing that suggests anyone else ever spent time here. No empty drawer once allocated for his clothes, no spot on the bathroom counter cleared for his things. He was never integrated into the space because she never planned for him to stay. This apartment is a fortress of solitude, and she kept it that way even when she wasn't alone.

**Third Floor Advantages:**
- No footsteps from above (top floor)
- Better natural light (higher up, less obstruction)
- Quieter overall (fewer neighbor interactions)
- Additional privacy (requires climbing three flights - discourages casual visitors)
- Slight exercise benefit (daily stair climbing)

**Building Context:**
- Small building (8 units total) = knows neighbors by sight, not by name
- No elevator = practical barrier to furniture acquisition, guests, deliveries
- 1960s construction = moderate sound transmission, single-pane windows, not energy efficient
- Mailboxes in lobby = daily stair descent required
- Street parking only = sometimes searches for spots near building

### What the Lab Means to Her

**Control and Competence:**
The lab is where Kate feels most capable. She knows exactly what to do, how to do it, and why it matters. The instruments don't judge her social awkwardness. The samples don't care if she's good at small talk. Results are objective - either the method works or it doesn't.

**Safety in Routine:**
Daily lab routine provides structure: Check instrument status, prepare samples, run analyses, process data, clean up. Predictable, measurable, controllable. Unlike human relationships, lab protocols have clear right answers.

**Social Buffer:**
The lab gives legitimate reason to avoid social situations ("Sorry, I have samples running"). It's professional cover for introversion. People respect lab commitments in ways they might not respect "I need alone time."

**Identity Anchor:**
When everything else feels uncertain (relationships, post-PhD plans, life direction), the lab remains constant. She's Dr. Morrison here (or will be). This work matters. This expertise is real.

## Running Routes - Daily Geography of Movement

### Primary Route: Lakefront Path (Northwestern Campus Loop)

**Distance:** 3.5 miles  
**Frequency:** 4-5 times per week (weather permitting)  
**Typical Time:** 6:30am weekdays, 7:00am weekends  
**Duration:** 28-30 minutes (8:00-8:30 min/mile pace)  
**Terrain:** Paved path, mostly flat with gentle hills  
**Seasonal:** Year-round (skips only in extreme cold, ice, or heavy snow)

**Route Details:**
1. Exit apartment, head east toward lake (5-minute warmup walk/jog)
2. Join Lakefront Path at campus edge
3. North along lake past Grosse Point Lighthouse
4. Loop through Northwestern campus (West on Sheridan Road)
5. South back to starting point
6. Cool down walk last 0.25 miles to apartment

**Why This Route:**
- Scenic (lake views, sunrise over water)
- Familiar (can run on autopilot, think about research)
- Safe (well-lit campus paths, other runners present)
- Convenient (starts/ends at apartment)
- Varied (views change with seasons, weather, light)

**Psychological Significance:**
This isn't just exercise - it's meditation, therapy, problem-solving time. She doesn't listen to music (wants to think). She doesn't run with others (solitary time essential). The route is memorized completely; she could run it blind. Her mind wanders to research problems, relationship processing, day planning.

The rhythm of running - breath, footfall, heartbeat - creates space for thoughts to untangle. After difficult days, she runs this route and returns slightly more settled. Not fixed, but manageable.

**Seasonal Variations:**

*Summer (June-August):*
- Earlier start time (6:00am to avoid heat)
- Lighter clothing (shorts, tank top, baseball cap)
- Water fountain stops (drinks mid-run)
- Faster pace (comfortable in warmth)

*Fall (September-November):*
- Golden hour runs (perfect temperature)
- Long sleeves, light gloves
- Favorite season for running
- Lake views particularly beautiful

*Winter (December-February):*
- Later start (7:00am, waits for sunrise)
- Full winter running gear (layered, hat, gloves, reflective jacket)
- Checks ice conditions before leaving
- Shorter runs if below 15Â°F or icy
- More treadmill runs at Northwestern gym (backup option)

*Spring (March-May):*
- Gradual return to outdoor running (winter recovery)
- Unpredictable weather (layers, prepared for rain)
- Muddy paths occasionally (adjusts route)
- Renewed energy (winter ending, summer approaching)

**Route Memories:**
This path has seen every version of Kate over three PhD years:
- First week of PhD: Anxious, uncertain, trying to prove herself
- Meeting John: Excited, hopeful, thinking about him while running
- Breakup aftermath: Devastated, running to escape thoughts (didn't work)
- Paper rejections: Frustrated, angry at herself
- Paper acceptances: Proud, validated, allowing herself brief joy
- Meeting Paul: Cautiously optimistic, processing new feelings

The lake doesn't change. The path stays the same. Kate keeps running. It's seen her stressed about comprehensives, excited about John, devastated by breakup, anxious about proposal defense, proud after paper acceptance. The path is unchanging (reliable) while her life keeps shifting. She returns to it like a touchstone, a place that makes sense when nothing else does.

## Coffee Shops & Third Places - Spaces Between

### Primary: Colectivo Coffee (Downtown Evanston)

**Address:** 716 Church Street, Evanston, IL 60201  
**Location:** Historic corner of Church & Sherman in downtown Evanston  
**Distance from Apartment:** 0.7 miles (15-minute walk, 3-minute drive in Clover)  
**Frequency:** 2-4 times per week (more in fall/spring, less in winter)

**Physical Space:**
- Interior: Industrial-modern design, exposed brick, high ceilings, large windows
- Seating: Mix of communal tables, small two-tops, armchairs, bar seating along window
- Natural light: Abundant from large storefront windows facing Church Street
- Atmosphere: Bustling but not loud, students studying, professionals on laptops, casual meetings
- Smell: Fresh coffee (rich, slightly sweet), baked goods, warm and inviting
- Sound: Espresso machine hissing, low conversations, indie music playing (never too loud)
- Temperature: Slightly warm (Kate usually removes jacket)

**Kate's Order:**
- **Standard:** Medium drip coffee, black ($2.95)
- **Occasional:** Small drip if already had coffee at home
- **Never:** Lattes, flavored drinks, anything complicated
- **Food:** Sometimes Morning Glory muffin (nut-free verified) or banana (backup if forgot breakfast)
- **Payment:** Debit card, sometimes has Colectivo gift card from mom (birthday/Christmas gift)

**Kate's Behavior Here:**
- **Arrival:** Enters, scans for empty table (prefers corner table by window if available)
- **Ordering:** Approaches counter when short line, orders same thing, brief smile to barista
- **Seating choice hierarchy:**
  1. Corner table by window (perfect - wall behind, view of street, not in traffic flow)
  2. Small two-top along wall (acceptable - wall support, relative privacy)
  3. Communal table end seat (tolerable if necessary)
  4. Bar seating along window (only if staying <30 minutes)
  5. Never sits: Middle of communal table, center of room tables, near bathroom

**What She Does:**
- Work: Reads papers, reviews data, writes (not primary workspace but good for focused reading)
- Think: Processes research problems, relationship dynamics, life questions
- Observe: People-watches without engaging, absorbs human presence without participation
- Exist: Sometimes just sits with coffee, allowing herself non-productive time (rare)

**Frequency Patterns:**
- Weekday mornings (10-11am): After arriving at lab, before diving into work
- Weekday afternoons (2-3pm): When needs break from lab, mental reset
- Weekend mornings (9-10am): Post-grocery trip, treating herself
- Never evenings: Colectivo closes at 6pm, Kate doesn't go after work hours

**Social Dynamics:**
Kate is a regular but anonymous regular. Baristas recognize her (same order, always polite) but don't know her name. She doesn't chat with other patrons. She doesn't make small talk beyond "thanks" and "have a good day." She exists in parallel to the social space without participating in it.

**Priya Exception:**
Only person Kate regularly meets here (1-2 times per month). When meeting Priya, Kate arrives early, secures preferred table, waits. Social anxiety slightly elevated but manageable (Priya is safe person). Conversation flows easier in public neutral space than private apartments.

**Why Colectivo Matters:**
Third places (not home, not work) are essential for mental health, even for introverts. Colectivo provides:
- Human presence without human demands
- Change of scenery without travel
- Permission to sit and think without productivity pressure
- Ambient social energy without participation requirement

She's learned that complete isolation makes her worse (spirals), but forced interaction exhausts her. Coffee shops offer middle ground - presence without participation. She can work, read, think, and absorb ambient human energy without giving any of herself away.

This is why Colectivo matters so much. It lets her be alone-together, which is what she needs most days. The coffee is excuse, the space is the point.
